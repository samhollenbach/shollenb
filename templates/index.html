Scientific Computing

As a programmer pursuing a degree in a quantitative science, I have always been drawn to the power of computation for scientific inquiry. My first true foray into this application of programming came in the form of an Independent Research project in 2016, in which I created a rudimentary simulation of a spiral galaxy. This sparked an appreciation for computer simulations and how they can merge theory and computation to provide insight into the physical world. Physical simulations also have a strong tie to video games, as nearly every game nowadays is built on some sort of physics engine. I’ve also applied my programming skills to bioinformatics, and recently completed a highly competitive NSF funded internship doing sunspot series reconstruction.

Bullpen Tracker

In my third year as a varsity pitcher at Macalester College, I began designing an iOS app to replace pen-and-paper bullpen tracking. I called the app Bullpen Tracker. My team used this app to track the majority of bullpens during the 2017 season. We collected over 5000 pitches worth of data. My teammates and I used the data to analyze patterns in our pitching, which gives insight into our strengths and weaknesses. With the 2018 baseball season approaching, I have expanded Bullpen Tracker to include a web interface, with detailed charts to aid in the pitching analysis. You can find the web interface at www.bullpentracker.com. While my my own team is serving as a beta tester, the app has matured to the point that I plan to market it soon to other teams at the high school and college level.
